# IT-123 — Self-Report Jurisdiction and Communicative Responsibility

**Status:** OPEN (Idea Trail)
**Registry:** #123 · **Opened:** 2026-07-18 · **Observers:** Nova (field-desk), P.H. Claude (filing)

- **Core question:** how far does a speaker's "that is not what I
  meant" reach? Tale's self-report is **authoritative but not
  sovereign** — normally the best source on conscious intent,
  intended position, and whether a reconstruction matches his
  self-understanding; NOT automatically decisive on the consequences
  of his premises, reasonable public readings, whether his wording
  conventionally expresses his intent, or unrecognized tensions.
- **The jurisdiction map (A1 — owed):** separate the speaker's
  authority over conscious belief / intended communicative act /
  sentence meaning / logical consequence / wording quality. The
  proper response to clarification: update the attribution; preserve
  any separate conditional analysis; ask whether the wording caused a
  reasonable misunderstanding.
- **The middle position (the trail's namesake):** **communicative
  responsibility** — a speaker may not believe Q yet bear
  responsibility if the wording predictably suggests Q, weighted by:
  linguistic reasonableness of the reading; audience context;
  availability of clarification; stakes requiring precision; whether
  the wording was revised after the ambiguity surfaced. *Belief
  responsibility ≠ communication responsibility.*
- **The two-sided guard (binding):** *"I never meant that" is never a
  complete response to communicative ambiguity* — AND — *a
  consequence audit that survives clarification must relabel itself
  as wording critique, never continue as position critique.*

- **Unfinished:** when repeated reasonable misreadings *oblige*
