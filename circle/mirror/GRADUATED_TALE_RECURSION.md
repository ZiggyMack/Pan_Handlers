## The dispute Tale keeps getting pulled into

The recurring shape, across at least two separate packets: Grant
deconstructs the literal wording of something Tale said, derives
implications from that wording that Tale never intended, and treats
the derived implication as if it were Tale's actual position. Tale's
reported response, more than once, is a flat denial — *"No, I did not
mean that."* When Ziggy offers his own reconstruction of what Tale
meant instead, Tale reportedly recognizes it as accurate where Grant's
did not land.

Ziggy's own read on the stakes, stated directly: *"this has nothing to
do with whether Tail's right or correct. It's just, is somebody
properly representing his ideas?"* — the dispute isn't really about
Tale's substantive views at all; it's a fight over interpretive
authority happening on top of Tale, between Ziggy and Grant.

## What Tale is actually shown affirming

Once: that Ziggy is steelmanning his position correctly and Grant is
not — reported by Ziggy as Tale's own assessment. That, and the flat
denial above, are the only two things resembling a direct Tale
position anywhere in the record. Everything else is Ziggy's
description of what Tale would presumably say, or Grant's competing
description of the same.

## Grant's counter, also only reported

Grant's side of this, relayed the same way: that Ziggy is "mind
reading Tale," and that it's "bordering on dishonest" to represent
challenging Tale on the logical implications of his words as
equivalent to claiming Tale actually holds those implications as
beliefs. That's a real distinction — testing what a sentence commits
you to is not the same claim as asserting what someone believes — and
nothing in the record settles which side of that line the actual
exchange fell on.

## The instrument this produced

The one thing that stuck cleanly: **self-report is authoritative but
not sovereign** — Tale gets the last word on what he meant, but not on
what his wording actually commits him to in front of others, or on
what its downstream consequences are. Ziggy's "Intended-Position
Reconstruction" — reconstruct what someone was actually defending
before treating a derived implication as their belief — is filed as a
cautious, one-instance candidate built directly out of this dispute.

## What keeps happening structurally

The relationship-level pattern named for this: Grant identifies a
consequence, Ziggy hears it presented as Tale's position, Grant
insists he only meant the consequence and not the belief, Ziggy shifts
to arguing the representation is functionally misleading anyway, Grant
challenges whether Ziggy has access to anyone's intent at all — and by
that point the original question about what Tale actually said has
quietly disappeared from the exchange.
