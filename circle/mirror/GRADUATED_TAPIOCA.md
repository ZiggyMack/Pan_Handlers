## The core point

Tapioca's contribution, consistently paraphrased across multiple
passes: there is no contradiction between holding that morality is
socially constructed and still calling someone morally bankrupt.
Social construction, on its own, doesn't undercut condemnation.

## Why it was ruled strong anyway

Despite the sourcing quirk above, the point itself held up under
scrutiny and got named as a binding finding: **anti-realism ≠
inability to condemn.** The move she's rejecting is a common one —
"if morality is just a social construct, you have no standing to
call anything bankrupt" — and her counter is correct as far as it
goes: constructing a moral framework socially doesn't strip it of the
ability to condemn things that violate it. What it doesn't settle is
the harder question underneath: what *authority*, *scope*, and
*binding force* that condemnation actually carries once you've
granted it isn't mind-independent. Her point clears the
consistency objection; it doesn't by itself answer the authority
question.

## The standing restriction

Her position only works if mind-independent categorical authority
isn't treated as the only real kind of normativity. Constructivist,
contractualist, sentimentalist, expressivist, and intersubjective
accounts of moral force all stay live alternatives — so nobody gets to
force a "realism or it's just preference" choice onto her position
either. That cuts both ways: it protects her point from an easy
dismissal, but it also means her point alone doesn't crown any one of
those alternatives as correct.
