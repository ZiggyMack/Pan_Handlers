## What the digs already imply

Confident, not corroborated — every claim traces to a real
T-DIRECT-SCREENSHOT source, but none has cleared this archive's actual
promotion bar; filed as packet-local positions, never operators.

**Compatibilism, stated plainly.** Tom's agency claim is direct and
unhedged: *"If the action comes from inside you that's agency even if
what's inside you was determined."* Determinism doesn't cost him
agency — it costs you the stronger claim that agency requires an
uncaused source. Ziggy's first-pass objection ("then moral
responsibility evaporates") overreached: hard determinism threatens
ultimate desert and sourcehood, not answerability or being responsive
to reasons. Tom's position survives that objection intact.

**The harm argument, laid out in sequence.** His full chain, in his
own words: morals don't exist in the universe, they're the rules a
functional society runs on — but that's not the same as "just his
opinion," because it's grounded in real harm. Harm is binding because
it supplies reasons to act. If suffering isn't a reason to act,
nothing is — including anyone's God. And if a conception of "good"
causes real suffering, it can't be good by definition.

That last move looks circular at first pass — defining "good" so that
it can't produce suffering, then using the definition to condemn
things that produce suffering. It isn't automatically circular,
though: stipulating a definition is not the same failure as assuming
your conclusion, and every framework's primitive axioms look
"definitional" from the outside. The open question isn't whether he's
begging the question — he probably isn't — it's what kind of force
his anti-realism is actually claiming: expressive disapproval,
intersubjective agreement, or something closer to categorical
obligation. He hasn't said, and "morals don't exist in the universe"
is compatible with any of the three.

**The unresolved regress.** Every conversation with Tom on this
territory runs the same shape: social rules get asserted, Ziggy asks
what authorizes them, harm gets named, obligation gets distinguished
from motivation, reasons get demanded, and a deeper grounding gets
asked for — until a definition gets supplied and the cycle either
stops or restarts one level down. No stopping rule has ever been
agreed on. He doesn't seem bothered by not having an answer to "where
does it end," the way some interlocutors are.

His exact metaethical home — error theory, noncognitivism,
relativism, constructivism, contractualism — has never been pinned
down and probably doesn't need to be for the positions above to hold.

**Status marker worth noting.** Tom's two seeds are filed as "the
archive's first non-Grant, non-Ziggy *member-level* direct seeds" — a
status none of Sorta, Kee, or Tapioca get, even though all four came
out of the same dig. He also gets his own dedicated offensive/defensive
role write-up, which the other three don't: offensively, he condemns
certain positions through harm and suffering, treating suffering as a
decisive symmetry-breaker; defensively, each refinement of his view
tends to introduce a new foundational commitment that isn't
independently defended — his definitional moves can function as
closure rather than explanation. And notably: **no interaction with
Grant is documented anywhere** — his exchange runs entirely against
Ziggy, in a separate thread from Grant's own material.

**The relationship pattern this produced.** Filed as the archive's
first Ziggy–Tom loop, the **Grounding Descent Loop**: Tom offers
social rules → Ziggy asks for authority → Tom appeals to harm → Ziggy
distinguishes obligation → Tom appeals to reasons → Ziggy asks for
deeper grounding → Tom supplies a definition — with no agreed stopping
rule anywhere in the exchange. The restriction cuts both ways: Tom's
grounding is informal-but-real, but Ziggy's own realist demand is
itself a commitment that owes grounds too.
